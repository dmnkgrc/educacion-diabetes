  • ADAPTER

    The adapter assists to keep the insulin cartridge secure inside the pump. It is the link (connector) between the pump, the insulin cartridge and the infusion set tubing. It keeps the cartridge compartment watertight with 2 seals, one inside and one outside the adapter. It holds the cartridge in place and prevents the entry of air bubbles. The 2 small vents on the adapter allow air pressure to be equalized inside and outside the cartridge compartment. The adapter will not work properly if the vents are blocked or dirty, or if the seals are missing. Note: It is essential to change the adapter at least every 2 months. You must also change the adapter if the seals become worn, are lost, or if the vents are blocked.

  • ADVANCED USER MENU

    One of three user menus offered by the Accu-Chek Spirit Combo insulin pump. This menu offers a variety of functions for insulin pump therapy with the ability to change between the user menus. The advanced menu opens up all 5 basal rates and the 4 bolus options. This is recommended for people who are experienced in pump therapy.

  • ADVICE OPTIONS

    The Advice Options settings ensure that the bolus advice takes into account insulin with remaining glucose-lowering activity from a previous bolus, thus preventing bolus stacking.

  • ALARM CLOCK

    The Alarm Clock function is a helpful way to remind you of daily events of your choice (from the list available) related to insulin pump therapy. You can set up to 8 reminders a day.

  • ANALOGUE INSULIN

    Currently, there are two types of insulins used in pump therapy, rapid-acting (analogue) and short-acting (regular human) insulin. Rapid-acting insulin analogs have the maximum effect in 1-2 hours after delivery.

  • AUTOMATIC OFF

    A safety feature of your pump that stops insulin delivery and triggers; Error E3: AUTOMATIC OFF; if no pump keys are pressed, or pump functions via the diabetes manager are not activated within a programmed time period in RUN mode. This feature can either be set to OFF or programmed up to 24 hours in 1-hour intervals.

  • AVERAGE

    The average is sometimes also called the 'mean'. Say you want the average score for 10 BG results. You would add the 10 individual results and then you divide this sum by 10, the number of results being averaged. The answer you get is the average or mean BG test result.

  • BACKLIGHT BUTTON

  • BASAL INSULIN

    People without diabetes have a constant, low level of insulin in their body between meals and during the night. This steady release of insulin is called basal insulin. Basal insulin levels go up and down depending on a number of factors. For example, when a person exercises, the body is more sensitive to insulin.

  • BASAL RATE

    When programmed correctly, your insulin pump delivers basal insulin every few minutes, 24 hours a day. This flow of insulin, measured in Units (U) per hour, is called the basal rate and is calculated by your healthcare professional to meet your basic insulin needs.

  • BASAL RATE PROFILE

    The basal rate profile of the Accu-Chek Spirit Combo insulin pump consists of up to 24 different hourly basal rates. Each hourly rate may be changed independently. You can set up to 5 different basal rate profiles on the Accu-Chek Spirit Combo insulin pump.

  • BASAL RATE TOTAL

    The sum of all 24 hourly basal rates in one basal rate profile is called the (daily) basal rate total. You will see the basal rate total when you first enter the BASAL RATE PROGRAMMING screen.

  • BATTERY COVER

  • BATTERY KEY

    This is supplied with the pump kit as well as the large and small service packs, and is designed to open and close the battery compartment.

  • BATTERY TYPE

    Lithium batteries: Only use lithium batteries that comply with IEC 60086-4. The Accu-Chek Spirit Combo insulin pump was successfully tested with an Energizer Ultimate Lithium FR06, AA, 1.5 V battery which complies with IEC 60086-4.

    Rechargeable batteries: In this case, we recommend a Varta Rechargeable Accu Ready to Use NiMH, HR06, AA, 1.2 V battery that complies with IEC 62133 and has a minimum capacity of 2600 mAh.

  • BG

    Blood glucose.

  • BG AVERAGES REPORT

    The BG Averages report lists averages and standard deviations of your BG results during a specific time period. For example, you can choose to include the results of the last 7, 14, 30, 60 or 90 days.

  • BG RESULT

    When you perform a blood glucose (BG) test, your diabetes manager shows a screen with your BG result. The diabetes manager also shows you how the BG result relates to your BG target using a colored result bar: blue with Hyper tag for Hyper warning, blue for above target range, green for within target range, yellow for below target range and red with Hypo tag for Hypo warning.

  • BG: STANDARD DAY REPORT

    The Standard Day report shows you averages and standard deviations for your BG test results for the categories Overall, Pre Meal, Post Meal, Bedtime and Others calculated for each Time Block of your Time Block setting. You can view them either as a table or as a graphic. You can choose whether the averages and standard deviations include the results of the last 7, 14, 30, 60 or 90 days.

  • BG: STANDARD WEEK REPORT

    The Standard Week report is organized by seven-day segments. It shows you averages and standard deviations for your BG test results for the categories Overall, Pre Meal, Post Meal, Bedtime and Others, calculated for each day of the week. You can choose whether the averages and standard deviations include the results of the last 7, 14, 30, 60 or 90 days.

  • BG TARGET RANGE

    Your blood glucose levels should be within this range to be considered normal before a meal. The upper and lower limits of the BG target range can be set with the diabetes manager Set-up Wizard. The limits of the target range can be set for each time block.

  • BG: TARGET REPORT

    The Target report displays the percentage of your BG results that are above or below your target range, and below your Hypo Warning Limit. It shows you the BG test results for the categories Overall, Pre Meal, Post Meal, Bedtime and Others. You can choose whether the percentages include the results of the last 7, 14, 30, 60 or 90 days.

  • BG TESTING

    A blood glucose test measures the amount of glucose (sugar) in the blood.

  • BG: TREND REPORT

    The Trend report combines the individual results for your BG test results for the categories Overall, Pre Meal, Post Meal, Bedtime and Others, with the entries for Carbs and insulin. You can choose whether the averages and standard deviations include the results of the last 8, 24 or 48 hours or 7 days.

  • BLOOD GLUCOSE

    Also known as BG. It is simple sugar (glucose) found in the blood. It is the body's main source of energy.

  • Bluetooth® WIRELESS TECHNOLOGY

    Bluetooth® wireless technology allows different digital devices to communicate to each other at high speed. The Bluetooth® wireless Technology sends and receives radio signals that connect your diabetes manager with your pump (like the remote control for a toy car).

  • BOLUS ADVICE

    When it is set up correctly, the bolus advice function in your Accu-Chek Combo diabetes manager can automatically calculate boluses and give you bolus advice. You then have the choice to accept the advice or not. If accepted, the bolus will be delivered by the pump. The calculations that drive the bolus advice function of the diabetes manager are based on a number of values pre-set on the meter and are based on carbs and blood glucose level.

  • BOLUS ADVICE SETTING: ACTING TIME

    The Acting Time is the total length of time the insulin has glucose-lowering activity after the start of the bolus delivery. At the end of the acting time, blood glucose, if elevated, is expected to have decreased to the target level and to have returned to the pre-meal level if covering a meal. You can adjust the length of the acting time to fit your individual needs, within a specified time interval (1½ hours to 8 hours).

  • BOLUS ADVICE SETTING: MEAL RISE

    During or after meals, an increase in blood glucose levels is considered normal within a certain range, even though a meal bolus has been delivered. A meal rise is in effect for a specified time period.

    Enter the maximum increase in your blood glucose result that is to be tolerated without an additional correction bolus.

  • BOLUS ADVICE SETTING: OFFSET TIME

    Offset time takes into account the expected delay for the blood glucose level to actually fall during the acting time of insulin in the body. It describes the first time period within the acting time.

  • BOLUS ADVICE SETTING: SNACK SIZE

    This is the maximum amount of carbohydrates in a meal that will be covered by a meal bolus which does not trigger a meal rise. After consuming carbohydrates that are equivalent to or are below the pre-set snack size value, no meal rise will be triggered. A post-meal correction bolus will always be recommended if the glucose level is above the target level. After consuming carbohydrates that are above the pre-set snack size value, a meal rise will be triggered. A post-meal correction bolus will only be recommended if the glucose level is elevated above the accepted increase.

  • BOLUS DATA

    This is the record of boluses delivered by the pump over the last 90 days. It is accessed from the RUN screen or STOP screen by pressing twice on the pump (or by pressing if you are using the diabetes manager).

  • BOLUS DOSES

    When nutrients are absorbed, the pancreas of someone without diabetes promptly releases ((or secretes)) extra insulin. In CSII therapy, this pattern is mimicked by delivering boluses to cover meals. The amount of insulin needed to cover nutrients is calculated using the persons' "Insulin-to-carb ratio" (your healthcare professional will assist work out what this is), therefore, you need to know how much carbohydrate is in the food that is eaten.

  • BOLUS HISTORY

    The record of boluses that have been delivered by the pump. The pump can store 4,500 events, or up to 90 days’ worth of history.

  • BOLUS INCREMENT

    You can raise or lower the bolus volume for the Quick Bolus by this amount each time you press a pump key or diabetes manager button. For example, if the increment is 0.5 units and you press the pump key twice, the total bolus volume will be 1.0 unit.

  • CALORIE

    A measure of the energy value of food.

  • CANNULA

    A type of hollow needle which is inserted under your skin. The cannula is a part of the infusion set. You have a choice of either steel or soft cannulas, sometimes referred to as a needle.

  • CARB RATIO

    This is the amount of insulin needed to account for a certain amount of carbohydrates. This will be calculated by your doctor and healthcare team and pre-set on the diabetes manager. It is also known as insulin-to-carb ratio.

  • CARBOHYDRATE

    Food is made up of carbohydrate, protein and fat. Sugar and starch are carbohydrates. Examples of foods rich in carbohydrate are cake, candies, lemonade (except sugar free), bread, porridge, potatoes and pasta. Carbohydrate is the main element that affects blood glucose.

  • CARBOHYDRATE COUNTING

    A method of meal planning for people with diabetes based on counting the amount of carbohydrate in food (in grams or carbohydrate units).

  • CARBOHYDRATE EXCHANGE

    See Carbohydrate Units.

  • CARBOHYDRATE UNITS

    Choosing how your carbohydrates will be measured is important for counting carbohydrates and for bolus calculations. In the diabetes manager Set-up Wizard, you can choose between: Grams; BE (≈ 12 g); KE (≈ 10 g); CC (≈ 15 g). Double check with your healthcare team the correct Carbohydrate unit for your region as once you have set this in the diabetes manager, you are not able to change it. If you set it incorrectly you will need to contact your Accu-Chek care line for assistance.

  • CARTRIDGE

    An insulin cartridge is the insulin reservoir for your insulin pump. The maximum amount of insulin the insulin cartridge can hold is 3.15 ml or 315 units of U-100 short-acting human insulin or U-100 fast/rapid acting analogue insulin. The insulin pump delivers insulin from the insulin cartridge into the infusion set tubing. Depending on the type of insulin you use, you will need to fill insulin from a vial into the plastic Accu-Chek Spirit 3.15 ml cartridge system. You can fully or partially fill the plastic insulin cartridge. Alternatively, if you use Insuman Infusat® 3.15ml pre-filled glass cartridges, you can insert these cartridges directly into the Accu-Chek Spirit Combo insulin pump.

  • CARTRIDGE SYSTEM

    The Accu-Chek Spirit 3.15ml cartridge system consists of an empty cartridge, a plunger and a filling aid. The system is designed to make it easy for you to fill your cartridge with insulin.

  • CARTRIDGE WAIT TIME

    The Cartridge Wait Time is the length of time in seconds that your pump will pause for while the cartridge is being changed or refilled. The default time is 300 seconds (5 minutes). If you find that this time is not long enough, it can be extended using the Accu-Chek 360° configuration software.

  • CHO

    Carbohydrate

  • CODE NUMBER

    Number printed on the code chip and the label of the test strip container which is displayed by the diabetes manager at the beginning of a test. The code number shows whether the code chip in the diabetes manager belongs to the test strips used. While code chip contains information about the test strips and always belongs to the test strips in the box with which it comes. The diabetes manager needs the information on the code chip to determine the test results.

  • CONTROL SOLUTION

    A solution with a pre-determined amount of glucose used in a control test. This test is to be done each time you open a new pack of test strips to check that your diabetes manager and strips are working well.

  • CONTROL TEST RESULT

    The result of the control test appears on your diabetes manager screen when you perform a control test. The result should be within the range shown on the test strip container.

  • CORRECTION BOLUS

    A correction bolus is different from a meal bolus. It is the insulin amount needed to compensate for a blood glucose level just measured, which is too high (above the target range). This is calculated using the persons “Insulin sensitivity factor” (your healthcare professional will assist work out what this is). BG levels can be elevated due to a variety of reasons; such as not enough meal insulin, stress, illness, medications or decreased activity.

  • CORRECTION FACTOR

    A formula that an individual can use to calculate the correct insulin amount needed to correct out-of-range blood glucose levels. The correction factor will be determined together with your healthcare professional.

  • CSII (Continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion)

    CSII is the term used for insulin pump therapy with the insulin infused under the skin. CSII therapy is designed to mimic the pattern of insulin secretion of the pancreas as it is in those without diabetes. A portable pump filled with rapid or short acting insulin is pre-programmed to continuously deliver a constant supply of insulin (in small bursts every few minutes) to the body every hour of the day and night (basal) via a subcutaneous cannula (under the skin). Additional doses are programmed as needed to cover carbohydrate intake (meal bolus) or to lower / correct elevated BG levels (correction bolus)¹. When using a portable insulin pump, there is no longer any need for injections (unless directed otherwise by your healthcare professional).

    ¹ Wolpert H et al. Smart Pumping for people with diabetes. A practical approach to mastering the insulin pump. American Diabetes Association. Alexandria, Virginia. 2000

  • DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS (DKA)

    Diabetic ketoacidosis is caused by a lack of insulin. Glucose levels increase and high levels of ketones build up in the blood, making the blood acidic. DKA can be life-threatening and requires immediate emergency medical care.

  • DISCONNECTABLE INFUSION SETS

    You can disconnect the infusion set tubing from the headset of the infusion set. This is useful when playing sports or during intimate moments.

  • DOWN BUTTON

  • DOWN KEY

    Use this pump key to move backward in the information screens, decrease a setting, turn off the STOP-Warning (press and hold 3 seconds until you hear a melody). The symbol used for this in the lessons is .

  • DUSK PHENOMENON

    At certain times of the day, our bodies release hormones, such as growth hormone, that counteract the effect of insulin. In people with diabetes, this leads to an increase in blood glucose. The Dusk phenomenon occurs later in the day, usually before dinner. High BG values in the afternoon are caused by insufficient basal insulin. The result is that the body needs more insulin to achieve the same effect during these times of the day. The insulin pump system allows you and your healthcare team to make basal rate adjustments to counteract this phenomenon.

  • ENTER BUTTON

  • ERRORS

    An Error informs you that you must respond NOW. For example, if Error E1 the cartridge is empty is shown, it means you must change the cartridge right away. See also Warnings.

  • ERROR HISTORY

    The record of any errors that have been reported by the pump. The pump can store 4,500 events, or up to 90 days’ worth of history.

  • EXTENDED BOLUS

    This is a type of bolus that is given by the pump over a specific period of time that is programmed by the user. This is especially helpful during long meals, or with high-fat meals that may delay a rise in your blood glucose level. People with delayed digestion, a condition called gastroparesis, may also benefit from an extended bolus. This bolus should not be used for a correction bolus. If you are programming a meal bolus to cover high fat, high carbs/long acting carbs and need a correction bolus at the same time, you should use a multiwave bolus instead.

  • FASTING

    Fasting blood glucose measures the blood glucose level after you have not eaten for at least 8 hours.

  • FAT

    Food is made up of carbohydrate, protein and fat. Lard, butter and salad oil are examples of high-fat foods. Fat is the second most important source of energy for our body. However, carbohydrate is the main element that affects blood glucose.

  • FILLING AID

    This is a part of the cartridge system and helps you fill new cartridges with insulin from a vial. It connects the plunger to the cartridge. When finished, it can be disposed according to local council regulations.

  • GASTROPARESIS

    Gastroparesis, also called delayed gastric emptying, is a disorder in which the stomach takes too long to empty its contents. Normally, the stomach contracts to move food down into the small intestine for digestion. The vagus nerve controls the movement of food from the stomach through the digestive tract. Gastroparesis occurs when the vagus nerve is damaged and the muscles of the stomach and intestines do not work normally. Food then moves slowly or stops moving through the digestive tract.

  • GLYCAEMIC INDEX

    This index ranks carbohydrates according to how quickly and how high they raise blood glucose.

  • GRAM (g)

    A measure of weight commonly used to assess the amount of carbohydrate, fat and protein in foods.

  • HEALTH EVENT: EXERCISE 1

  • HEALTH EVENT: EXERCISE 2

    There are two exercise health event options to allow you to adjust boluses based on, for example, mild and strenuous exercise periods. This function needs to be pre programmed in the diabetes manager and should be set under the guidance of your healthcare professional.

  • HEALTH EVENTS

    This is part of the bolus advice setting. You can use Health Events to take into account situations that increase or decrease your insulin needs, such as exercise, illness, stress or the menstrual cycle. For example, when exercising, your insulin needs may be lower. The Health Events function allows you to lower your bolus amount by a certain percentage in this case. The Health Event options need to be pre programmed in the diabetes manager and should be set under the guidance of your healthcare professional.

  • HEART PROBLEMS

    People with diabetes are at higher risk of developing high blood pressure, heart attacks and strokes.

  • HIGH BG (HYPERGLYCAEMIA)

    Also called high blood sugar or high blood glucose. This results from having insufficient insulin in your body. You may need to take additional insulin and/or measure your urine for ketones.

  • HUMAN INSULIN

    Currently, there are two types of insulins used in pump therapy - rapid acting (analogue) and short acting (regular human) insulin. Human insulin is the same as regular human insulin: it has the same chemical structure as the insulin delivered by the pancreas of a person without diabetes. The peak action of human insulin tends to take place between 2 and 4 hours after delivery.

  • HYPER WARNING LIMIT

    If your blood glucose is above this limit, the BG test result will show a blue bar with Hyper tag and a Hyper Warning. The Hyper Warning limit is set with the diabetes manager Set-up Wizard. Check with your doctor or healthcare team about the Hyper Warning limit that is right for you.

  • HYPERGLYCAEMIA (HIGH BG)

    Higher than normal blood glucose (BG) levels, results from having insufficient insulin in your body. You may need to take additional insulin and/or measure your urine for ketones.

  • HYPO WARNING LIMIT

    If your blood glucose is below this limit, the BG test result will show a red bar with a Hypo tag and a Hypo Warning. The Hypo limit is set with the diabetes manager Set-up Wizard. Check with your doctor or healthcare team about the hypo limit that is right for you.

  • HYPOGLYCAEMIA (LOW BG) hypo

    Hypoglycaemia is a condition characterized by abnormally low blood glucose (blood sugar) levels. It is important to talk to your healthcare professional about your individual BG targets and what level is too low for you. Hypoglycaemic symptoms are important clues that you have low BG levels. Each person's reaction to hypoglycaemia is different, so it's important that you learn your own signs and symptoms when your blood glucose is low and how to manage it.

  • INFRARED INTERFACE

    The built-in infrared interface on the bottom of your Accu-Chek Spirit Combo insulin pump enables you to transfer data between your pump and a computer. For details, see the user information provided with the software.

  • INFUSION SET

    The infusion set enables insulin to be transferred from the cartridge inside your pump to the subcutaneous tissue of your body. When we talk about the "infusion set" we are normally referring to the 2 major components of the set = 1 / A head set that houses the cannula and 2 / A transfer set with the tubing / line. The cannula is made of either Teflon® (soft cannula) or stainless steel (steel cannula). A soft cannula is inserted with an introducer needle which is then removed once the cannula is in place. A steel cannula does not require an extra introducer needle for insertion. The infusion set is used together with a protective cap/ protective cover for situations when the pump is disconnected from the body, e.g. showering, playing contact sports, scuba diving etc. Depending on the type of infusion set, the cannula can be inserted at a variable angle or at 90 degrees. Soft cannulas are normally changed every 2-3 days and steel cannulas every 1-2 days. Depending on manufacturers recommendation; tubing or line is generally changed with every cartridge or at least every 6 days.

  • INFUSION SITES

    Places on your body that are suitable for infusing insulin. The best places are around the belly, but choose a site away from the waistline, bones, scar tissue, belly button and recent infusion sites.

  • INJECTION THERAPY

    Insulin administration under the skin by insulin pens or syringes. If repeated doses of short- and long-acting insulin is injected over the 24 hours, this is referred to as MDI (Multiple Daily Injections). See glossary entry under MDI.

  • INSERTION DEVICE

    Some pump users prefer to use an insertion device, like the Accu-Chek LinkAssist automatic insertion device, to insert the infusion set cannula rather than manually inserting it by hand. The device has a spring-loaded design that inserts the cannula into the skin quickly and virtually pain-free. The simple design of the Accu-Chek LinkAssist automatic insertion device allows you to use only one hand if needed as well as allows you to visibly see where you are inserting your cannula in to.

  • INSULIN

    Insulin regulates the use and storage of sugar (glucose). Insulin acts like a key. It opens the doors to the body cells to let glucose enter.

  • INSULIN INCREMENT

    see Bolus increment.

  • INSULIN PUMP

    A small, computerized, programmable device about the size of a beeper that can be programmed to send a steady stream of insulin into the bloodstream as basal insulin, as well as larger amounts prior to meals as boluses. It replaces insulin injections.

  • INSULIN SENSITIVITY FACTOR

    Insulin sensitivity factor is the amount of insulin needed to lower your blood glucose by a certain amount. It is also referred to as “correction factor”. This will be calculated by your doctor and healthcare team and pre-set on the Accu-Chek Combo diabetes manager.

  • INSULIN-TO-CARB RATIO

    This is the same as Carb ratio. The amount of insulin needed to account for a certain amount of carbohydrates. This will be calculated by your doctor and healthcare team and pre-set on the Accu-Chek Combo diabetes manager.

  • INSULIN VIAL

    A small container, usually made of glass, often used to hold medicines for injections. In this case, it holds your insulin.

  • INTRODUCER NEEDLE

    This is a steel needle inside a soft cannula. It helps you to insert the soft cannula by keeping the cannula stiff. The introducer needle is then removed after insertion.

  • KETONES

    Ketones are chemicals that the body creates when it breaks down fat to use for energy. The body does this when it doesn't have enough insulin to use glucose - the body's normal source of energy. When ketones build up in the blood, they make it more acidic. When ketone levels are too high, diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) can develop. You can "self-measure" your ketones and your Healthcare professional will guide on you how much insulin is needed depending on the result shown.

    Used to prick your finger (palm, or area designated by your healthcare professional) to get a drop of blood for a BG test.

  • LIPOHYPERTROPHY

    Tissue build up that may develop when you inject insulin too many times into the same area. The sites on the body where the infusion set is placed should be rotated regularly to avoid lipohypertrophy.

  • LOW BG (HYPOGLYCAEMIA)

    Also called low blood sugar or low blood glucose levels. Low BG can occur when you take too much insulin, eat too little food, drink alcohol or exercise. Ask your healthcare team for appropriate action to take in response to a low BG.

  • LOW QUANTITY

    The Low Alarm Quantity is the level of insulin at which your pump signals that the amount of insulin in cartridge is low. The level at which the alarm is triggered can be altered to suit your own circumstances, using the Accu-Chek 360° configuration software.

  • LUER LOCK CONNECTION

    Also referred to as Luer-lock. It is the fitting on the end of the infusion set and on the cartridge that allows them to be connected, or locked together securely. This type of connection is often used in medical devices.

  • MAXIMUM HOURLY BASAL RATE

    The Maximum Hourly Basal Rate is the maximum amount of insulin that can be delivered by the insulin pump each hour. Your pump delivers basal insulin every few minutes. You can change the amount of insulin being delivered for any hour but only up to this maximum figure.

  • MEAL BOLUS

    When nutrients are absorbed, the pancreas of someone without diabetes promptly releases extra insulin. In CSII therapy, this pattern is mimicked by delivering boluses to cover meals. The amount of insulin needed to cover nutrients is calculated using the persons “Insulin-to-carb-ratio” (your healthcare professional will assist work out what this is), therefore, you need to know how much carbohydrate is in the food that is eaten.

  • MEAL INSULIN

    Short-acting insulin or rapid-acting insulin that is taken before, during, or just after a meal which contains carbohydrates to help control blood glucose levels.

  • MEAL TIME ENTRY: BEDTIME

    In the Meal Time field on the BG Result screen you can flag the test result as Bedtime for BG test you performed before going to bed. You can then choose to view your BG results In the My Data menu on the diabetes manager for Bedtime only. It shows your averages and standard deviations for your BG test results at bedtime. You can choose whether the averages and standard deviations include the results of the last 7, 14, 30, 60 or 90 days.

  • MEAL TIME ENTRY: OTHER

You can choose whether the averages and standard deviations include the results of the last 7, 14, 30, 60 or 90 days.

  • MULTIPLE DAILY INJECTIONS (MDI)

    Multiple daily injection / multiple dose injection therapy is an alternative term for the "basal/bolus" regime of injecting insulin. This therapy involves injecting long or intermediate acting insulin once or twice daily as background (basal) dose and then having a further injection of rapid or short acting insulin (bolus) dose with each meal to cover any carbohydrate intake and or elevated BG levels. MDI therapy will usually involve at least four injections a day. Additional injections of rapid or short acting insulin may also need to be injected outside of meal times to lower elevated BG levels. Injections can be given via pen, syringe or an injection device.

  • MULTIWAVE BOLUS

    This bolus combines an immediate bolus delivery followed by an extended bolus delivery. It can help to better match insulin delivery to individual insulin requirements, e.g. when having high carb-high fat meals.

  • NEPHROPATHY

    Diabetic nephropathy is a long term complication of diabetes. It is caused by damage to the small blood vessels in the kidneys.

  • NEUROPATHY

    Diabetic neuropathy is a long term complication of diabetes. It affects the nerves of the body, especially the feet and legs.

  • PAIRING

    Pairing provides a unique connection between your diabetes manager and pump. If you have already acquired your diabetes manager together with the Accu-Chek Spirit Combo insulin pump, the two devices will already have been paired. If you acquired the devices separately, you must pair the devices before they can communicate with each other. Only one diabetes manager at a time can be paired with your pump.

  • PANCREAS

    A gland positioned near the stomach that secretes insulin, glucagon and many digestive enzymes.

  • PLUNGER

    This is a part of the cartridge system and helps you fill new cartridges with insulin from a vial. It is like the plunger on a syringe and is connected to the cartridge by the filling aid. When filling is finished, it is removed from the cartridge and can be disposed according to local council regulations.

  • PRIME QUANTITY

    The Prime Quantity represents the amount of insulin in units that will be pumped into the infusion set to clear it of air. The correct Prime Quantity amount will depend on the length of the infusion set being used. The Prime Quantity amount can be altered to suit the length of tubing / line you use by adjusting the pre programmed amount using the Accu-Chek 360° configuration software.

  • PRIMING THE INFUSION SET

    When a new cartridge is inserted, when there are air bubbles in the cartridge and/or after changing an infusion set, the tubing/line needs to be primed (filled with insulin). The infusion set is properly primed when you see no air bubbles in the tubing and a bubble-free flow of insulin emerges from the tip of the cannula.

  • PROTEIN

    Food is made up of carbohydrate, protein and fat. Lean meat, soy and dairy products are examples of food rich in protein. Protein foods have little effect on the blood glucose.

  • PUMP

    A small, computerized, programmable device about the size of a pager that can be programmed to send a steady stream of insulin into the subcutaneous tissue as basal insulin, as well as larger amounts prior to meals as boluses. It replaces insulin injections.

  • PUMP KEYS

    The pump has four keys. On the face of the pump are the menu key and the OK key. On the side of the pump are the down key and up key. The functions of each of these keys are covered in further detail in the glossary.

  • QUICK BOLUS

    A quick bolus is a standard bolus (a single dose of insulin given all at once). It can be easily programmed with a preset bolus increment per key-press. It can be programmed using the Up and Down keys without looking at the screen. See your User's Manual for more information on how to do this.

  • QUICK INFO

    This is the summary of the status of your pump. It shows you how much insulin is left in the cartridge, what type of insulin bolus you are using (if you have an extended or multiwave bolus running) and the current signal settings (beep, vibration or both). It is accessed from the RUN screen or STOP screen by pressing the OK key once on the pump (or by pressing the right button once if you are using the diabetes manager).

  • REMINDER

    Reminders help you to remember a variety of diabetes-related tasks. For example, the diabetes manager can help remind you to test your BG, when to change your infusion set and when to visit the doctor.

  • RETINOPATHY

    Diabetic retinopathy is a long term complication of diabetes. It affects the small vessels of the eye and can lead to vision problems including blindness.

  • ROTATION GUIDE

    This useful guide helps you to decide where to insert your infusion set next. You need to change or rotate your infusion sites to prevent infections and inflammation. There are several recommended patterns, such as zigzag or circular. A new infusion site should be at least 5 cm (2 inches) away from the last one. Ask your healthcare team for further advice.

  • RUN MODE

    During normal use, your Accu-Chek Spirit Combo insulin pump is in RUN mode and is continuously delivering insulin.

  • SOFT CANNULA

    Soft cannulas may be more comfortable to wear than steel cannulas as you don’t feel the cannula when bending or twisting. Infusion sets using soft cannulas need to be changed every 48-72 hours.

  • STANDARD BOLUS

    It is a single dose of insulin given all at once. Use this bolus prior to eating food that includes a fast acting carbohydrate (like cake or fruit) or for correcting high BG values/correction bolus.

  • STANDARD DEVIATION

    The standard deviation is a number that tells you how tightly your BG results are clustered around the mean (or average). When the test results are tightly bunched together, the standard deviation is smaller and when the test results are spread apart, the standard deviation is larger.

  • STANDARD USER MENU

    One of three user menus offered by the pump. This menu offers all the basic functions you need for insulin pump therapy with the ability to change between the user menus. This is recommended for people who are new to pump therapy.

  • START-UP PROCESS

    After you change the battery on your pump, it goes through a series of tests to check it is working properly. This is called the start-up process.

  • STEEL CANNULA

    Steel cannulas are smaller in diameter than soft cannulas and are often used by people who find soft cannulas difficult to insert. Infusion sets using steel cannulas need to be changed every 24-48 hours.

  • STOP MODE

    When your Accu-Chek Spirit Combo insulin pump is in STOP mode, it does not deliver any insulin.

  • SUBCUTANEOUS (FATTY) TISSUE

    There are three main layers of the skin. Epidermis - this is the top layer of the skin, the part of the skin you see. Dermis - is the second layer of skin. Subcutaneous fat - is the bottom layer. This is where your cannula will be inserted into.

  • SYNCHRONIZE

    Your pump and diabetes manager synchronize the time and event history when they communicate. That is, the diabetes manager adopts (takes over) the time shown on the pump and updates event history files.

  • TEMPORARY BASAL RATE (TBR)

    You can program your Accu-Chek Spirit Combo insulin pump to temporarily increase or decrease your basal rate. This function is helpful in matching your changing insulin needs due to increased or decreased activity level, illness or stress. Your basal rate is regarded as 100%. You can, however, increase the basal rate to as high as 250% or to as low as 0% for up to 24 hours. When you increase or decrease your basal rate, each hourly rate for the duration you program is increased or decreased.

  • TEST STRIP

    Test strips are used with your diabetes manager to work out your blood glucose level. A small drop of blood is put onto the yellow window of a test strip that has been inserted into the diabetes manager. The diabetes manager is then able to detect the amount of blood glucose present in your blood.

  • TIME BLOCK SETTINGS

    Time Blocks split the day into time periods, to help better manage the programming for insulin delivery settings that may change throughout the day. Examples include insulin sensitivity and carb ratio.

  • TIME BLOCK SETTINGS: CARB RATIO

    You must program a Carb Ratio for each Time Block you wish to set up. It is used to calculate a meal bolus.

  • TIME BLOCK SETTINGS: INSULIN SENSITIVITY

    You must program Insulin Sensitivity for each Time Block you wish to set up as your insulin requirements may change over the course of the day.

  • TIME BLOCK SETTINGS: TARGET RANGE

    This is one of the parameters you can set for each Time Block. It is used to create a target BG for that Time Block. The target is calculated automatically as the average between the lower and upper BG values you program for each Time Block.
